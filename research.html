Who Should Get Money? Estimating Welfare Weights in the U.S. (with Francesco Capozza)

Welfare weights measure the value of providing an additional dollar of consumption to individuals in society. We identify the welfare weights assigned by the general population of the U.S. to individuals in society, using a large-scale real-stakes experiment. The weights obtained from our experiment are sufficient statistics that can incorporate various fairness ideals and can be directly used in policy settings to evaluate whether a reform is desirable or not. In our experiment, participants in the role of ``Social Architects" assign welfare weights to seven real "Recipients" with after-tax incomes that span the income distribution of the U.S. We find that the welfare weights of the general population (i) exhibit, on average, a low degree of progressivity, (ii) exhibit considerable heterogeneity, (iii) are stable across various income groups and political affiliations, and (iv) are similar to the weights implied by the existing policies in the U.S. We explore the implication of the general population weights for the optimal income taxes and the optimal sugary drinks taxes.
